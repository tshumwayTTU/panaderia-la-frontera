Our story

Three generations, one kitchen.

It started with a notebook of recipes carried up from Chihuahua, and it's still the same hands at 4 AM.

Carried up from Chihuahua

In 1971, Rosa Hernández crossed from Chihuahua to El Paso with two trunks of clothes and her mother's notebook of bread recipes. She opened the first Panadería La Frontera with a single oven and an alarm clock set for 3:30 AM.

The conchas she scored that first morning were from a recipe her mother learned from her grandmother, who had baked them in a wood-fired horno outside Parral. The recipes haven't changed since. Same butter ratio. Same proofing time. Same hand on the scoring knife.

Three generations of hands

The bakery is run today by Rosa's daughter Lupita and her grandson Mateo. Lupita took over in 1998 after her mother retired. Mateo handles the early shift now, scoring conchas while Lupita focuses on the rosca and seasonal breads.

Three pairs of hands have shaped the dough that comes out of this kitchen. Same recipes, three generations of muscle memory, one quiet kitchen at 4 AM.

Why we still bake by hand

We could buy a machine. They make 400 conchas an hour and never get tired. We score by hand because the cracks in the sugar shell are different every time, and that matters. A concha you score yourself remembers your hand. A concha from a press is just a number.

We bake because Rosa baked. Because the recipes are family. Because El Paso deserves a panadería that smells like one.

Panadería La Frontera is a fictional bakery created for an academic project. No real business is implied.
